Why I Disable Syntax Highlighting▮▮▮▮

2023-11-03

Syntax highlighting!

Beautiful rows of vivid colour, arrayed across the screen like a field of flowers, shining in the golden glow of the sun.

And bane of my existence.

At some point... I realised I could better comprehend code without the extravagant colours. And it makes perfect sense—we don't colourise any other text, not prose nor poetry nor mathematical notation (unless we do).

So how exactly did syntax highlighting become the default?

Most people do seem to appreciate the "feature" however, regardless of my personal preference. But here I'll explain at least my own rationale for disabling syntax highlighting everywhere.

Reason #1: Sensory Overload

In today's day and age we're barraged by information all day every day. Painting code all colours of the crayons is but another wholly unnecessary stream of useless information to take in. Is there really a need to light up every other word like a Christmas tree, when the syntax itself conveys perfectly well whatever message the colours are supposed to?

Reason #2: Parallel to Paper

Text on paper is always much more readable than on a screen—easier on the eyes, no backlight, visible in daylight; the list goes on.

Black on white (or white on black) provides the best contrast you could need, making for so much greater legibility. And being monochrome we can display the code on an e-paper display for daylight viewing. Perhaps at some point I may build a cyberdeck with an e-paper screen specifically for coding and reading.

Additionally we can fax the code or print it with a printer, for whatever reason you might want to do that...

Reason #3: Look Less Suspicious

When people look over your shoulder and look at your code and see nothing but 12px black and white bitmap glyphs on an xterm... they're not going to care. Show them your circus of syntax and suddenly you're a hacker. It's a painful experience programming...
