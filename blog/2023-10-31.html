On Synthetic Biology, Computer Science, and Capitalism▮▮▮▮

2023-10-31

Happy Halloween!

Today I will be writing about the scariest thing of all: the future.

Before I start, I'd like to say that I'm far from an experienced engineer or biologist, nor am I educated in politics or economics at all, beyond my debates with my friends - so do take my words with a grain or five of salt. If you disagree with anything I've written here, feel free to drop me an email :)

o o o

One of the most egregious aspects of modern times in the western world - and rife especially in the technology industry - is the abundant promotion and normalisation of the consumerist lifestyle.

Three months ago, my friend dropped his iPad and broke its screen. Instead of going down the route of repair, he opted instead to purchase an entirely new iPad - when I asked him why, he stated that it was simply "quicker" than to deal with fixing the device.

To me this seemed absurd; as someone who has always at least attempted to repair broken machinery, what benefit could there be to depleting our limited finances so wastefully?

In a word, "convenience".


A widely touted adage in business - as far as software is concerned - is to simplify the process for people to give you money. When it becomes more convenient for a customer to hand over their money than to spend any measure of effort to achieve the same result by any other means, such shall they do.

The result, as it happens, is that companies optimise for this to the point where the most convenient solution is rarely the best one (for anyone not directly profiting from the company itself, in any case). Going back to our example of the iPad, there's been a great bedlam about the right-to-repair surrounding Apple; the company's general hostility towards their customers having the audacity to think that they should be capable - and by extension permitted - to repair their own devices, is really quite incredible, and has resulted in the production of a wholly ridiculous quantity of e-waste.

But how did we get here? By what mechanism has it come to be, that technology as advanced as the iPad - with such vast numbers of transistors, composed into a system so complex that no one person could truly understand its entirety - is commoditised so?

One such mechanism, is the concept of abstraction. Abstraction is wonderful; it enables us to build beautiful constructs of parts, each of which we may not quite understand, and each of which, may further be composed of but more parts. This is a foundational concept in computer science, with the field itself built in its entirety upon it: from physics, electronics; from electronics, logic circuits; logic circuits, computers; computers, more computers. And it all culminates in the consumer device. No-one really knows how a smartphone works - but we certainly do know how to use one, for better or for worse.

Inevitably, this abstraction is used for profit. The past 5 decades have seen the rise of the web, and with it, its exploitation by and for corporate interest. In this way has the advancement of computers been accelerated to an incredible velocity - the interest of industry has funded the research and machinery necessary to design, manufacture, and sell denser, better, faster computers.

Where abstraction becomes a problem, is where neither businesses nor consumers have any awareness of the underlying technology, such that we begin playing with what could only be described as magic. Magic, however, is inscrutable. Useful, but dangerous. When no-one knows what's going on inside, how can we validate that there is no venomous spider lurking within? Open-source helps to alleviate some of these issues - but we are far from a world of open software, let alone hardware. Thankfully, unlike venomous spiders, computers can't physically attack and envenomate us, however.


Living beings on the other hand, certainly can.

The field of synthetic biology in its current state is frequently compared to the early stages of the computer industry. Abstraction is similarly used to compose complex systems from a library of parts, such as logic gates and switches. At present this abstraction is not quite so clean, given the incredibly complex interactions between chemical species alone, but there is considerable progress already: we have CRISPR, entire operating systems for biocomputing, DNA data storage, the total synthesis of E. coli - a myriad of advancements made every year.

In future, it is feasible for there to be such massive levels of abstraction and commoditisation - as is already prominent in the computer industry - that a layman in their living room could order an algorithmically designed custom organism to their door; or specialised medicine, synthesised by but another small appliance beside the kettle.

The dangers of this are of course innumerable - viruses and biological weapons abound, trivial to design, manufacture, and sell. Perhaps by then we may have developed immunity to such things, by means of technology or otherwise, but it is impossible to predict such things. For this reason, it is typical of governments the world over to fiercely dictate biological technologies on the scale of all of individuals, businesses, and researchers.

What must be avoided, is if it should come to be that biology is made entirely inaccessible to the individual - in this way would we begin to head towards a world where we should see corporations exploiting people for monetary gain through the use of synthetic biology, with the individual being powerless against it. A parallel in the computer industry would be the modern Internet.


The most widely used browser as of today, is Google Chrome. Chrome is used by the vast majority of people, most of whom are unaware of the inane amounts of personal data collected and stored in Google's servers. This data is used by Google to produce targeted advertisements, which are sold for profit.

The collection of this data being Google's primary source of income then, is its main motivation for, of course, collecting more data. This comes at the expense of the privacy of its users, and should be considered a highly unethical practice. There are of course laws surrounding this, such as the GDPR - but when users "agree" (typically unknowingly, by terms and conditions and bright blue "I agree" buttons) to this collection, there is little opposition, especially when such exploition generates significant monetary gain.

There is further an environmental impact to this: the immense quantity of data being stored and processed by Google alone requires quite ridiculous amounts of energy - and for what? Does the blatant disregard for privacy, for the sole purpose of bringing about targeted advertisements each of which too, is designed to manipulate people towards further consumerism, bring some meaningful benefit to society?

Due to Chrome's massive usage, Google has the impetus to implement as many unethical practices into its software as will earn more money. A recent attempt at just such a thing, was the Web Environment Integrity (WEI) API, which was met with such opposition that Google was fortunately convinced to cancel its development.

What makes this significantly dangerous then, is if such attempts were to be successful - there was no legal obligation for Google to cancel the WEI. It is fortunate then, that there exist open-source alternatives to the likes of Google Chrome, Apple iOS, and Microsoft Windows. Such alternatives can be used by individuals (like myself) who are sufficiently concerned about their freedoms and privacy. An excellent example of one such open-source project, is the Linux kernel and its family of operating systems.

The point is, we can imagine a world where the development of software were to be restricted solely to licensed corporations - the notion of open-source software would be but a pipe dream, leaving individuals with their only options being to be exploited by any number of companies, or to forgo such technology entirely.

In synthetic biology, we might consider a similar situation - should individuals be restricted from practising biology, we would see the rise of a world where proprietary commercial biological technologies become the norm, monopolised by a select few companies, each prioritising financial gain over ethics. Perhaps a corporation might sell some medicine, essential for survival, at a blithely marked-up price, owing to them owning the sole patent or licence for its production.


The term "biohacking" has come to describe many things, one of which being DIY biology - that is, the movement promotive of improving the accessibility of biology/biotechnology to individuals and small organisations.

The continued legality of biohacking will be key in the prevention of such a society - one example in recent times is the small-scale production of insulin. For many afflicted with diabetes, in areas of private healthcare, insulin is simply not affordable - despite being essential for survival. There is no financial incentive to reduce the price of insulin, so companies choose not to do so - people will buy it anyway after all. Or else die.

For this reason, individuals have contributed to developing small-scale, open-source production pathways for affordable insulin.

Likely, if the barrier to entry regarding licences and such were lower, we might have had a far greater number of willing contributers to a variety of similar projects. This competition from open-source would then provide ample motivation for companies to reduce prices; back to the idea of convenience, if companies were to make prices low enough such that the convenience of purchase outweighed the effort required for a DIY solution - there would then be motivation to produce affordable medicines.

Again however, we can expect companies to then still optimise for profit - perhaps selling personal data gained from medical history, perhaps targeting advertisements towards those most vulnerable to some illness that they sell the product to treat, perhaps bypassing safety protocols and exploiting legal loopholes to cut costs of production.

In such a world, there must be - at the very least - the open-source alternative.
